who is this man?

An old newspaper that seems to be all about business. With on the the cover Christophe Plantin the Owner and founder of The Officina Plantiniana.

Plantin's Passport

The Ceo, Christophe Plantin, was born in the region of Tours, France. His exact birth date is unknown, but most likely somewhere around 1520. His early years are vague . His parents died when he was 15—leaving him all alone in France without any money. He turned to learning bookbinding and met his future wife, Jeanne Rivière.

They had five daughters together. In France, they quickly realised they had no chance of making it big in this country. To successfully fulfil his dream of becoming a printer, he moved to Antwerp, where the story of his printing empire started.

Business Bills

A business guide written by a poor businessman is worth nothing. Plantin has extensive experience in the business industry; his qualifications are no joke . While he has had many ups and downs, his glory days in 1568 and 1572 prove he is capable as a businessman.

He had to start over from scratch nearly three times, but in the end, he built a successful empire that lasted nine long generations and was the leading business in the field of printing for over 300 years.

achievements

At its most successful time, his company had 22 presses and more than 80 employees. Under all these employees was a group of printers, compositors, proofreaders and various servants. Together, they helped Plantin print as many books as possible. No other printing company had as many presses as him, with even the most famous printing houses at the time not having more than four. The scale of his business was gigantic.

His book with the most copies printed was his Hebrew Bible in 1566

business techniques

Logo of Labour

How he brought it this far is no mystery; his business techniques are straightforward and understandable. Do keep in mind that while these techniques helped build his company to the heights it achieved, the most important part of his business might have been "Labore et Constantia”, the motto of his business and life. He could not have managed to make it this big had he not pushed through in hard times and continued working even then.

A Family Business

If you want to set up a business, you need employees. Preferably, people who will stay loyal to you and won't ask for too much money. Plantin quickly realised that the best employees were right there, his five daughters. From the age of four, his daughters learned to read and write, a necessary skill for working in his printing company. When they had mastered reading in all languages, they were set up as proofreaders.

On the Map

When he moved his whole family from France to Antwerp, he didn't just throw a dart at a map and hope for the best. No, Antwerp was a strategically chosen city. At the time, Trade in Antwerp was booming. Thanks to the internationally well-used port, the city was flourishing. Many artists and entrepreneurs from all over Europe came to Antwerp, now the mercantile hub of Europe.

Antwerp was the place to be for starting a new business. Plantin took advantage of this and set up his printing business.

In Colour?

Plantin tried to sell as many books as possible. One way he did this was by catering to as many different people as possible. He targeted both the rich and the poor. One of the big differences between his cheaper books and the most expensive books was in the images. Plantin had perfected using either etchings or woodblocks to print the illustrations in his books.

However, no matter which technique he used, he could still only print in black or red. This meant that if he wanted coloured illustrations, he had to hire a painter to colour the illustrations for him. And thus, only the rich could have the privilege of buying a book with coloured illustrations.

Precarious Pitfalls

Bankruptcy

Plantin also encountered a few pitfalls during his many years of running his business that any business owner should try to avoid. These next things are better avoided to avoid endangering your business with bankruptcy.

Precarious Pamphlets

Something that should be avoided is what happened to Plantin when he was away on business in France. During a raid on his shop, a Calvinistic pamphlet was discovered. No matter if Plantin was the person who printed it or not, his whole company was publicly sold.

He had no choice but to stay in Paris until his name was cleared, lest he be executed. And upon his return, all his furniture had been sold. He only narrowly managed to start up shop again because those who had bought all his stuff were his friends willing to collaborate with him.
